## TL;DR

### An Unlikely Path to Self-Discovery

Joe describes his early life as a period of intense, almost constant self-abuse, driven by a brutal inner critic. He wasn't on a conscious "path" but was a self-proclaimed rebel with a "fuck the system" mentality. This rebellion fueled a deep curiosity about humanity, leading him to collect as many diverse experiences as possible.

- He would intentionally seek out obscure situations, from drinking with cowboys in a trailer park bar to hanging out with homeless people and billionaires in the same week.
- He saw this experience-gathering as an agenda-free investigation into what makes people tick, spurred on by a multicultural upbringing that showed him how much of life is just cultural programming.
- "I was trying to understand humanity in any way that I could," he explains, suggesting this drive may have originated from the need to "read" his alcoholic father as a survival skill.

A classic example of this phase was his "sun ritual" in Alaska. Inspired by a book on a Lakota medicine man, he hitchhiked to a fish cannery to endure hardship. The work was grueling, 16-hour days of butchering fish, which led to a long-term aversion to cooked fish and a game called "gut tag."

### The Awakening and The Work

The first inkling of a conscious path came from realizing he had a "relationship with himself." The true turning point, however, was a meditation retreat at age 26 where he had an eight-second experience of oneness. For years afterward, his path became a desperate attempt to get back to that feeling.

He later realized that the "head awakening" from non-duality and meditation wasn't enough. He had peace, but not joy. His body felt unsafe, a place he'd been taught to ignore. The real change began when he embraced embodied, emotional work.

- He found his most effective teachers by spotting someone who had radically changed and asking, "What the fuck happened to you?"
- This led him to seven years of intense Reiki and breathwork with a woman who would physically push on his muscles to release stored trauma. "I'd come out bruised often," he recalls. This work was crucial for unlocking his emotional and physical being.
- He learned that breakthroughs always came from welcoming a difficult emotional experience, grieving the old way of being, and facing the fear of "who will I be if I change?"

### From Meditation Cushion to Venture Capital

Joe's move into the business world was another unexpected turn. At 35, the need to provide for his new family forced him to figure out how to make money. The entry point was surprisingly simple: while working as a video artist, he gave some casual financial advice on gold and oil to a wealthy client who made a fortune. The client then offered to fund whatever Joe wanted to do.

He pitched two ideas: a non-profit to help kids with consciousness and a venture fund for environmental causes. This work led to a profound realization: the root of environmental problems isn't a lack of technology, but human ego. This insight shifted his focus entirely to helping people understand and love themselves, believing it was the key to solving bigger world issues.

### How a Bad Investor Became a Great Coach

Here's the surprising part: Joe admits he was a terrible venture capital investor. His identity was wrapped up in being needed, so he invested in founders who needed his help. "What you really want to pick is people who don't fucking need you," he says. Because his investments were struggling, the only way he could add value was by coaching the founders on consciousness and self-awareness.

This necessity became his calling. People started seeking him out for coaching, and what began as a way to save his failing investments turned into his life's work. He had always resisted being a "teacher," fearing he'd get stuck and stop evolving, but he fell into it backward. As he jokes, "Capitalism has made me what I am today."

### The Current Edge: From Dissolution to Permeability

Now facing growing influence, Joe is navigating the challenge of "audience capture"—being shaped by the attention he receives. For years, his method for staying grounded was the dissolution of self, seeing the work as flowing through him, not from him. This prevented it from becoming an ego trip.

However, he's realizing that to avoid losing empathy as his platform grows, he needs to shift from dissolution to permeability of self. It's no longer about a disappearing "me," but a more spacious "me" that can be touched by others' experiences.

"To be intimate with that experience is incredibly personal," he says, reflecting on the daily messages of gratitude he receives. He believes that allowing himself to be personally moved and have his heart broken by these stories is essential to staying human and present in his work.
